About Synthetic Spinel

Synthetic spinel is a man-made gemstone prized for its brilliance, durability, and versatility. Originally developed as an alternative to natural gemstones, synthetic spinel is created in a laboratory environment, most commonly using the flame fusion (Verneuil) process. It is available in a wide range of vivid colors and is often used as a simulant for sapphire, aquamarine, and other gems. Its excellent optical properties and ease of cutting make it a favorite among faceters and collectors alike.

Chemical Formula: MgAl2O4
Crystal System: Cubic (Isometric)
Refractive Index: 1.727–1.740
Specific Gravity: 3.58–3.61
Hardness (Mohs): 7.5–8.0
Dispersion: 0.020
Typical Colors: Colorless, blue, pink, red, green, yellow, violet
Transparency: Transparent to opaque
Cleavage: None
Fracture: Conchoidal
Luster: Vitreous
Fluorescence: Usually inert, but some colors may fluoresce under UV
Synthesis Method: Flame fusion (Verneuil process)

Faceting Notes:

  • Polishes well with alumina or cerium oxide on tin or Lucite laps.
  • Sharp facet junctions; takes a high polish.
  • Low risk of heat damage during cutting.
  • Careful orientation can maximize color and minimize windowing.

Collector Notes:

  • Often used as a simulant for sapphire and other gems.
  • Readily available in large, clean crystals.
  • Distinguishable from natural spinel by inclusions and growth features.
